Tort Law: Psychiatric Injury

A visual strategy guide for mastering psychiatric illness claims in negligence.

Phase 1: The General Hurdles

Before analyzing who the victim is, the court applies two strict filters. First, as per Hinz v Berry, the harm must be a medically recognized illness. Second, as per Alcock and Walters, it must result from a sudden "shock to the system," rather than a gradual accumulation of grief or stress.

Recognition of Illness (Hinz v Berry)

The visualization above contrasts actionable clinical conditions against non-actionable emotional states. In exams, always verify if the "trauma" meets the clinical threshold.

1. Medical Recognition

Actionable: PTSD, Pathological Grief, Personality Disorders.

2. Sudden Shock

Must be a "sudden appreciation by sight or sound." Walters extended this to a 36-hour continuous event.

Exam Tip: If the scenario doesn't explicitly name a condition, state: "It is assumed that medical evidence will substantiate a clinical diagnosis."

Phase 2: Victim Classification

Determining the "duty of care" depends on whether the claimant is a Primary or Secondary victim. According to Page v Smith, if a claimant is in the physical danger zone, their threshold for claiming is much lower than those witnessing from afar.

Primary Victims (Zone of Danger)

  • Direct participants in the accident.
  • Rescuers (only if in physical danger - White).
  • Unwitting Agents (believe they caused death - Dooley).

Secondary Victims (Witnesses)

  • Not in physical danger zone.
  • Witnesses the event or immediate aftermath.
  • Must satisfy all Alcock Control Mechanisms.

Foreseeability Standards

This chart compares the "Legal Bar." Primary victims only need physical harm to be foreseeable to claim for mental harm. Secondary victims must prove psychiatric harm was specifically foreseeable.

Phase 3: The Alcock Control Mechanisms

For secondary victims like Tina, the court applies three strict "Control Mechanisms" to prevent indeterminate liability. Failing even one of these results in a failed claim.

❤️

Close Link

Close tie of love and affection. Presumed for spouses/parents; others must prove it (Siblings, etc.).

📍

Proximity

Spatial and temporal closeness to the event or its "immediate aftermath."

👁️

Perception

Direct perception through unaided senses (seeing/hearing). Excludes TV/Third-party news.

The Cumulative Nature of Alcock

The Radar chart illustrates that a claim only succeeds when the "area" covers all three axes fully. If proximity is zero (e.g., finding out via phone), the entire claim collapses.
